Conclusion

Based off the information detailed in the Results section of this memo, we have concluded that drastically reducing fixed utility charges, while slightly increasing variable utility charges, adding a separate charge for high usage which predominantly affects higher-income households, and increasing utility bill assistance for lower-income households, can result in significant relief from cost burden for millions of lower- to medium-income citizens, without higherâ€“income households experiencing energy cost burden and allowing the utility provider to make a reasonable level of profit. As one can see from Table 3 and Table 4 in the Results section, the combined charge from electricity and gas on high-consumption households in the first, lowest income quintile decreased from a burden of 18.03% of annual income after taxes, to a percentage of 5.97% of annual income after taxes, which satisfies the requirement that low-income households pay no more than 6% of their annual income towards utility bills.

Modifications to HEAP benefits were required along with modifications to utility rate structure to avoid imparting cost burden from electricity and gas payments. Average annual HEAP benefits were increased to $1090, an increase of 2,344% from $46.50, the average annual quantity of HEAP benefits distributed to New York City households[4]). This tool has the potential to be used by both lawmakers and utility providers, possibly in direct cooperation, for the benefit of lower-income households who may be adversely impacted by utility cost burden.
